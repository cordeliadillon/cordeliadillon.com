Principles of Universal Design

Core Principles

  • Developed in the 1990s at the Center for Universal Design, NC State University
  • Funded by the National Institute on Disability and Rehabilitation Research
  • Spanning multiple areas (architecture, public spaces, products, digital spaces)

Core Principles

  1. Equitable use
  2. Flexibility in use
  3. Simple and intuitive use
  4. Perceptible information
  5. Tolerance for error
  6. Low physical effort
  7. Size and space for approach and use

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

1. Equitable use

The design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities.

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

1. Equitable use

  • Provide same means of use for all users: ideally identical, at least equivalent
  • Avoid segregating or stigmatizing users
  • All users should have same privacy, security, & safety rights
  • Design something people want to use!

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

2. Flexibility in use

The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities.

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

2. Flexibility in use

  • Provide choice in methods of use
  • Facilitate the user's accuracy and precision
  • Adapt to user's pace
  • Be input device agnostic*

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

3. Simple & intuitive use

Use of the design is easy to understand, regardless of the user's experience, knowledge, language skills, or current concentration level.

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

3. Simple & intuitive use

  • Remove unnecessary complexity
  • Be consistent with user expectations
  • Be consistent with structure and hierarchy of page
  • Plan for wide range of literacy & language skills
  • Prompt and give feedback during and after user actions

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

Content is key

  • Proper heading outline
  • Plain language
  • Helpful link text
  • Descriptive image alt text

Consistent navigation patterns

Consistent navigation patterns

  • Semantic HTML
  • Persistent landmarks
  • Consistently labelled elements

4. Perceptible information

The design communicates necessary information effectively to the user, regardless of ambient conditions or the user's sensory abilities.

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

4. Perceptible information

Make sure essential information...

  • Is conveyed in multiple modes
  • Has good contrast
  • Is as legible as possible

Also...

  • Differentiate elements in ways that can be described
  • Provide compatibility with AT used by people with sensory limitations

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

5. Tolerance for error

The design minimizes hazards and the adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions.

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

5. Tolerance for error

  • Arrange elements to minimize accidents or errors
  • Provide warnings when appropriate
  • Provide fail safe features
  • Discourage accidental actions in important flows

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

6. Low physical effort

The design can be used efficiently and comfortably and with a minimum of fatigue.

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

6. Low physical effort

  • Avoid physical strain*
  • Use reasonable operations
  • Minimize repetitive actions
  • Minimize sustained physical effort

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

7. Size and space for approach and use

Appropriate size and space is provide for approach, reach, manipulation, and use regardless of user's body size, posture, and mobility.

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

7. Size and space for approach and use

  • Plan for multiple sizes of hands
  • Give enough space for AT and assistance

— NC State University, The Center for Universal Design

Proximity

  • Size and placement of interactive elements

    • Not so far that user has to scroll

Initial Brainstorm (30)

  • Sort yourselves into groups of 3-5.
  • Get a product brief:
    http://cordeliadillon.com/talks/sydney-universal-design/generator/
  • 5 min: Think up ideas individually, as many as you can. Feel free to sketch and take notes.
  • 20 min: Share ideas with your group.
  • 5 min: As a group, pick 2-3 ideas to iterate on.

Iteration (30)

Start working on wireframes or outlines of your feature sets. These can be really loose! As you work, consider:

  • What assumptions are you making about your users?
  • How would the personas from A Web For Everyone interact with your product?
  • What would they find easy or confusing?
